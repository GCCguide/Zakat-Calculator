What is Zakat?

Zakat is the third pillar of Islam — an obligatory annual charity paid by eligible Muslims on their accumulated wealth. The word "Zakat" means both purification and growth, reflecting its dual spiritual and social role.

Allah says in the Quran: "Take from their wealth a charity by which you purify them and cause them to increase" (At-Tawbah: 103). Zakat purifies one's wealth and soul while redistributing resources to those in need.

Key fact: Zakat is not a tax or a loss — the Prophet ﷺ said: "Charity does not decrease wealth" (Muslim). It is one of the most powerful tools for social welfare in Islamic civilization.

Conditions for Zakat to be Obligatory

Zakat becomes obligatory when all of the following conditions are met:

ConditionExplanation
Being MuslimZakat is an obligation for Muslims only
Reaching NisabWealth must meet or exceed the minimum threshold
One lunar year (Hawl)Wealth must have been held for a full Islamic year
Full ownershipThe wealth must be fully owned and accessible
Free from debtDebts due within the year can be deducted

Nisab: The Minimum Threshold

Nisab is the minimum amount of wealth a Muslim must possess before Zakat becomes obligatory. There are two standards:

Gold Nisab

85 grams of pure gold (24 karat). To find the Nisab in your currency: multiply 85 × today's gold price per gram.

Silver Nisab

595 grams of silver. Many contemporary scholars recommend using the silver Nisab as it is more inclusive and benefits more poor people.

Example: If gold is $60/gram, the Nisab = 85 × $60 = $5,100. Anyone with wealth above this threshold held for one lunar year must pay Zakat.

Types of Zakatable Wealth

1. Cash & Bank Savings

All cash on hand, current accounts, savings accounts, fixed deposits, and money lent to others. Zakat rate: 2.5% on the full amount.

2. Gold & Silver

All forms including jewelry, bullion, and coins. Personal jewelry worn regularly is disputed among scholars — paying Zakat on it is the safer (precautionary) position.

3. Business Inventory (Trade Goods)

Stock and goods held for trade are valued at current market price. Subtract business debts due within the year, then pay 2.5% on the net.

4. Stocks & Investments

For trading stocks: pay Zakat on full market value. For long-term investment stocks: pay Zakat on the Zakatable assets of the company proportional to your shareholding. The simpler approach is to pay on full market value.

5. Rental Income & Real Estate

Your home is exempt. Property held for sale: pay on market value. Rental property: pay on net rental income received (not property value).

Step-by-Step Zakat Calculation

Step 1: Add all Zakatable assets (cash + gold + trade goods + stocks + rental income).

Step 2: Subtract debts that are due within the current year.

Step 3: Compare the net total to the Nisab. If it meets or exceeds Nisab, proceed.

Step 4: Multiply the net total by 2.5% (or divide by 40).

Example: Cash $20,000 + Gold $8,000 + Stocks $12,000 = $40,000. Minus debts $5,000 = Net $35,000. Zakat = $35,000 × 2.5% = $875.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I pay Zakat on my monthly salary?

Zakat is not due on salary the moment it's received. However, any portion saved and held for a full lunar year alongside other wealth is subject to Zakat when the total reaches Nisab.

Is Zakat due on debts owed to me?

Yes — money others owe you that is likely to be recovered counts as Zakatable wealth. Debts you owe are deducted from your Zakatable total.

When should I pay Zakat?

At the completion of one lunar year (Hawl) from when your wealth first reached Nisab. Paying early is permitted; delaying without reason is not.

Can I give Zakat to relatives?

Yes, to eligible relatives who are not your direct dependents (children, parents). Giving to a needy sibling or uncle is valid and earns double reward — charity and maintaining family ties.

What is the Zakat rate?

The standard Zakat rate on monetary wealth is 2.5% (one-fortieth). Agricultural produce has different rates (5% or 10%), and livestock has separate Nisab rules.
